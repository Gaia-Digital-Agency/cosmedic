A funnel review

The funnel,
considered.

BIMC CosMedic v1.1 is editorially mature — the brand reads as a sanctuary, the surgeons are credentialed, and pricing is unusually transparent for the category. What it lacks is a measurable spine: clear conversion events, a tighter awareness-to-enquiry path, and a few quiet pieces of friction-removal in the middle of the funnel. Below, an honest assessment and twelve specific changes — each one yours to accept or decline.

Five honest findings
Finding I

Awareness is editorial; intent is not measurable.

There is no visible analytics surface (events, UTM strategy, scroll depth, form‑abandonment). Every recommendation downstream depends on knowing where the leak is — today, the team is guessing.

Finding II

The hero asks for a leap, not a step.

Hero quick form is two fields, but the framing ("Plan Your Treatment") presumes commitment. Cold visitors are 6–8 weeks earlier in the buying cycle than that copy assumes. They need a smaller next step.

Finding III

Procedure pages are unconverted.

40+ procedure pages carry the long tail of organic traffic, yet they share one generic CTA ("contact"). No price‑estimator, no 'compare to home country', no surgeon‑specific booking. This is the highest‑intent surface in the funnel and the least optimised.

Finding IV

Pricing is honest but cognitively expensive.

Three named packages (Refresh / Edit / Composition) require interpretation. International patients comparing Bali to Sydney want one number, in their currency, for their procedure — not a poetic tier name.

Finding V

Trust signals are present but front‑loaded.

ACHSI, ISAPS, FICS and 4.9‑star average appear on the homepage trust strip. They do not reappear on procedure pages, contact form, or pricing — exactly where doubt peaks in the journey.

Finding VI

The international‑patient calculus is invisible.

The single biggest objection ("Why Bali, vs. my own surgeon in Sydney/Singapore/London?") is never answered head‑on. No savings calculator, no flight+villa+procedure total, no "what does this cost at home" comparison.

Add a "Calculate your saving" module on the homepage.

A short interactive: select procedure → home country → see Bali total (procedure + flights + 14‑night villa) vs. local equivalent. Becomes the single biggest reason a cold visitor reads on.

№ 03
Interest → Consideration Effort · Medium · Impact · High

Add a smaller next step alongside "Plan Your Treatment".

A second CTA throughout the site: "Get a price estimate in 60 seconds" — a 4‑question wizard (procedure, home country, timing, contact) that returns a banded price by email. Captures cold visitors who aren't ready to enquire but will surrender an email for a number.

A single big CTA loses 60–70% of the visitors who would have converted on a smaller one.

Consideration Effort · Low · Impact · High

Convert procedure pages with three local CTAs.

Each procedure page (40+) currently funnels to a single "contact" link. Add three contextual CTAs in the right rail: (a) "Book a video consult with the lead surgeon", (b) "Get a written estimate for [procedure]", (c) "WhatsApp the concierge about [procedure]". Each pre‑fills the destination form.

Procedure pages are your highest‑intent traffic. Today they have the same generic CTA as the homepage.

№ 07
Intent Effort · Low · Impact · High

Shorten the contact form to three required fields.

Current form has 9 fields. Reduce required to: name, email, area of interest. Everything else (country, dates, travelling‑with) becomes optional and progressive. Each removed required field typically lifts completion 5–10%.

Friction at the form is the single most measurable conversion lever on any site.

Decision Effort · Medium · Impact · High

Build an automated post‑enquiry sequence.

The moment someone submits the form, send (a) instant confirmation with a calendar link to book a video consult, (b) the Bali Recovery Guide PDF, (c) a 30‑second intro video from the lead surgeon, (d) a 24h follow‑up if no booking. Today: silence until concierge replies.

High‑intent leads cool 50% in the first hour. Automation holds the heat while concierge prepares a tailored response.

Decision Effort · Medium · Impact · Medium

Add a "Book a video consult" path with self‑service scheduling.

Embed a calendar (Cal.com / Calendly) for a free 20‑minute video consult with a coordinator — no surgeon time required. Lets high‑intent visitors lock a date without writing an enquiry. Funnels them into the same automated sequence afterwards.

A booked call is worth ~10x an unbooked enquiry. Self‑service removes the email tennis.

№ 10
Trust · Cross‑funnel Effort · Low · Impact · Medium

Replace static testimonials with verified video reviews.

Three written testimonials on home are good but undifferentiated. Two 30‑second video testimonials (one rhinoplasty, one hair) tagged with the verification source (Google review, Trustpilot ID) outperform any quantity of text quotes. Keep the rest as supporting evidence.

Video is the highest‑bandwidth proof in cosmetic medicine; written quotes are baseline hygiene.

Measurement Effort · Low · Impact · Foundational

Instrument the funnel with named events.

You cannot improve what you do not measure. This unlocks every later optimisation.

Retention → Referral Effort · Medium · Impact · Medium

Build a quiet referral loop into aftercare.

At month 3 of the 12‑month telehealth follow‑up, send a simple, brand‑appropriate referral note: "If a friend asks where you went, this is the link to send them." Track via unique referral codes. Cosmetic medicine is the highest‑word‑of‑mouth category; today there is no system catching it.

A satisfied patient at month 3 is the most credible source of new enquiries you have. Make sharing effortless and tracked.
